In 1931, Kurt Gödel published a proof that shook the foundations of mathematics. Within a decade it had escaped the seminar room and entered the culture — where it has been misunderstood ever since.

The incompleteness theorems — there are two — are genuine landmarks of human thought. But the conclusions popularly drawn from them bear almost no relation to what Gödel actually showed.

## What the Theorems Say

The first incompleteness theorem states, roughly, that any consistent formal system powerful enough to express basic arithmetic must contain true statements that cannot be proved within that system. The second states that such a system cannot prove its own consistency.

These are precise, technical results. Their proofs are models of clarity. And yet they have been recruited to support claims about the limits of human reason, the impossibility of artificial intelligence, and the existence of God — none of which follow.

## The Misreadings

The most common error is to conclude that Gödel showed mathematics is *incomplete* in some global, devastating sense — that there are mathematical truths forever beyond our reach. But the unprovable statements Gödel constructs are highly artificial. They are not the kind of thing mathematicians were trying to prove.

A subtler misreading holds that the theorems show human minds can do something computers cannot. This was the argument of the philosopher J.R. Lucas in 1961, and it has been revived periodically ever since. The response, developed by logicians including Hilary Putnam, is devastating: the argument proves too much and rests on an equivocation about what it means to "know" a statement is true.

## What Remains

Strip away the misreadings and something genuinely important remains. Gödel showed that the formalist programme — the dream of reducing all mathematics to a single, complete, consistent formal system — is impossible. That is a significant result. It means mathematics is not, and cannot be, a closed book.

But it does not mean mathematics is arbitrary, or that truth is relative, or that human intuition is irreducible. It means that mathematical knowledge is open-ended in a specific, technical sense.

That is remarkable enough without the mythology.
